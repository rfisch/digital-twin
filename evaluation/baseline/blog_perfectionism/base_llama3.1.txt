Letting Go: When Perfectionism Becomes a Creative Prison

You know that feeling when you're working on a new piece, and every sentence, every word, feels like it's being ripped from your soul? Like you're pouring your heart onto the page, but at the same time, questioning whether it's good enough? That's perfectionism talking – and I'm here to tell you that sometimes, perfectionism needs to take a backseat.

I used to be the queen of perfectionism in my writing. Every word had to be precisely placed; every sentence had to flow like honey; every paragraph had to be chock-full of profound insights (yawn). But the thing is, this level of scrutiny sucked the joy right out of me. I was stuck in a creative prison, too afraid to take risks and try new things.

So, what's the first step towards breaking free? Acknowledging that perfectionism is not only unnecessary but also suffocating. It's like trying to cook the perfect soufflé – it might rise to great heights initially, but eventually, it'll collapse under its own weight. You have to let go of the need for precision and control.

Now, I'm not saying you should just slap together a half-baked manuscript (although, let's be real, that can be therapeutic in its own right). What I am saying is that you need to find a balance between striving for excellence and giving yourself permission to make mistakes. It's okay if your writing isn't perfect – it's supposed to be messy, imperfect, and utterly human.

You have to trust the process, not just your writing self, but also your inner editor (the one who's always whispering "this needs work" in your ear). Trust that the words will flow when you're ready; trust that the story will unfold as it should. And for goodness' sake, don't be too hard on yourself.

Here's a question to ask yourself: what's the real cost of perfectionism? Is it worth sacrificing your creativity and joy on the altar of "better"? Or can you find a way to let go, just a little bit?

Take a breath. Let go of that need for control. And remember, your writing doesn't have to be perfect – it just has to be yours.
